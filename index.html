The Ritual of Reflecting

I wanted to peel away the layers of reflection in order to reflect on reflection itself. What's happening in the very moment my foot hits the pavement?

I suffered during my first few sessions. I barely completed 2 miles before (what I thought was) my body begged to stop.

I started noticing my mind, without any concrete goal or form of entertainment to distract it, wandering around to find something to play with. My thoughts, without music or television, jumped to neurotic estimates of my strides per minute equating to a rough minutes-per-mile figure. My reflection, without a timer or pedometer, strained to make itself out in the window in front of the treadmill.

The mind, bored and agitated, would tell me it's time to stop running. My body was fine, though. I learned to start observing my breath as it entered through my nose and my mouth into my chest. I grew to understand how connected my breath was to my heart, my shoulders, my core, my hips, hamstrings, knees, calves, and ankles.

Within a couple weeks, I started losing track of how long I had been running for. I began breathing exclusively through my nose, reducing the number of breaths I took per minute, and trying my best to keep my heart rate and stress levels down.

I began to enjoy running.
